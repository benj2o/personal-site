Entrepreneur First was a useful reminder that founder judgment often only shows up under pressure. Everyone invited to the London selection days is pre-selected as the top one percent of talent. Everyone looks exceptional on a resume. Yet you still see a lot of founder cosplaying. This is the act of being a founder only to be a founder. Knowing the right advice and giving great speeches with nothing to back it up. 

You can tell very quickly who actually belongs in a room like this once you start working together over a few hours. I made three positive and one negative prediction on who would advance from my immediate group. I was right on all accounts. The environment rewards people who move before they have perfect information and update rapidly when they are wrong.

I flew into Luton on Sunday morning straight from a birthday the night before. I caught the Germany vs. Curacao game on the way to a friend's house. Great game. I missed about half the goals. The next morning featured the usual introductory speeches, and then everyone was let loose.

I had already shortlisted the cohort beforehand. By the time the second speech ended, I was working with a friend, S.H., on the logistics behind radiopharmaceuticals. I knew the space reasonably well from the 2024 Petten crisis and thought the supply chain was a serious problem worth solving. We spent most of the day testing the viability. By late afternoon we both realized we had been working too closely for too long and were better off splitting up to maximize our surface area at the event.

We tried a few more ideas together first. Hashable document verification for court evidence got killed quickly once we realized the space was heavily saturated by digital signatures.

Then there was a water leak in the building. We started pulling numbers. The UK loses somewhere between £700 million and £1 billion a year to domestic water damage. Fewer than 7% of households have any kind of sensor installed. That kind of gap should make you immediately suspicious. We spent the rest of the day calling sensor manufacturers, engineering firms, a research lab working on the IP behind external sensors, and existing clients. We needed to understand why distribution was so low.

The answer was reasonably clean. Public buildings and large offices already require advanced detection by code. That segment is saturated. Insurers occasionally bundle sensors for high net worth individuals buying property. They rarely do it otherwise because the return on investment does not work at scale. The average consumer experiences a leak once every six to eight years. That is rare enough for normalcy bias to do most of the work. The problem is real. The willingness to pay is absent.

A few things I am taking away from the event.

Learn to let go of ideas quickly. Even the ones you care about. Radiopharmaceuticals matter. The point of EF is to compress the search. Running more ideas with more people beats refining one prematurely.

Be paranoid when something looks easy. A clear problem with a clear solution and low adoption is almost never an opportunity hiding in plain sight. There is usually a reason. You want to find it before you commit.

Know exactly who your customer is. A problem existing does not mean anyone will pay to have it solved. A company that cannot find paying customers will not survive long enough to fix the problem you care about.

Starting a company is easier than people think. Starting a good one is harder than people think. EF makes the difference between those two obvious very quickly. I liked that about it.

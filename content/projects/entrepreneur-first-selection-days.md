Entrepreneur First is an international business incubator and venture capital firm that supports individuals in building technology companies.

### Intro
{.reflection-heading}

EF was a useful reminder that founder judgment often only surfaces under pressure, which applies to both myself and others. Everyone invited to London is already pre-selected as the top 1% of talent and looks exceptional on a resume. Nevertheless, you immediately get the impression that certain people are clearly more or less potent than others. Just by observing, I made four out of four correct guesses on who would proceed to the next round.

I also noticed a significant amount of founder and investor cosplaying in the current industry. This involves acting as a founder or VC merely for the sake of the title. It includes reciting the right advice, giving rehearsed speeches, and casually overusing terms like "Building" and "Shipping" alongside words like "Cracked", "Democratizing" and the constant defeatism in regards to politics and the EU, which I find insufferable.

### Sunday (Day before)
{.reflection-heading}

I flew into Luton on Sunday morning straight from a birthday the night before and found accommodation at a friend's place for the next few days. Sunday night, I met up with two people from EF and watched the Germany vs. Curaçao match. It was a great game, although I missed half of the eight goals.

### Monday
{.reflection-heading}

The next morning featured the usual introductory speeches before everyone was let loose.

I had already shortlisted the cohort beforehand. By the time the second speech ended, I was working with a friend, S.H., on the logistics behind radiopharmaceuticals. I knew the space reasonably well from the 2024 Petten crisis, where the High Flux Reactor was temporarily shut down and close to 50% of the global supply was lost. I considered the supply chain a serious issue worth solving. We spent most of the day calling various contacts and repeatedly pivoting. None of our concepts seemed plausible given the highly regulated market, the entrenched existing players, and the unclear path to profitability. Identifying customers proved exceptionally difficult. This held true for our downstream ideas as well, such as software or enabling remote access for 99mTc-DPD scans to check for cardiac amyloidosis.

By late Monday, we realized we had been working too closely for too long in the hope of forcing a solution, and that we were better off splitting up. Our early momentum had caused us to rest on our laurels before the rest of the room even started. We recognized that the true benefit of the event was maximizing the number of people and ideas we engaged with to develop our ideation muscle, rather than being blinded by an attachment to a specific issue.

S.H. and I could easily resume working on radiopharmaceuticals on any other occasion if it proved commercially viable.

### Tuesday
{.reflection-heading}

A prior founder carousel helped me refine my shortlist further. I chose to work with K, a former software engineer from Goldman Sachs. He suggested several ideas, including datasets for betting markets and hashable document verification for courts and insurance. I dismissed these concepts quite quickly.

We took a short break for food and noticed a water leak in the kitchen, which prompted us to investigate. We started pulling numbers. The UK loses between £700 million and £1 billion annually to domestic water damage, yet fewer than 7% of households have any kind of sensor installed. That disparity should make anyone paranoid. We spent the next hour calling insurance companies, sensor manufacturers, engineering firms, a research lab working on the IP for external sensors, and existing clients to understand why distribution remained so low.

The answer was straightforward. Public buildings and large offices already require advanced detection by code, meaning that segment is saturated. Insurers occasionally bundle sensors for high-net-worth property buyers, but they rarely do so otherwise because the return on investment does not scale. The average consumer experiences a leak once every six to eight years, which is infrequent enough for normalcy bias to take over. As a result, even though the problem itself is significant, there is virtually no consumer willingness to pay for a preventative solution.

The last person I worked with, J, previously worked at NASA and Rolls-Royce and shared my strong interest in aerospace. He was developing a model that offered substantial improvements over standard CME predictions, which I believed had strong commercial potential. Building on the previous document verification concept, I also proposed a point-of-capture proof system for aviation, which could eventually scale to space as orbital infrastructure expands. Every hour a plane stands on the ground costs the airline around £xk, with roughly x minutes of that time dedicated to safety walks. Speeding up this process using drones and cameras would reduce airline costs while providing verifiable proof for insurance firms, airports, and airplane lessors, who own 50% of the fleet, in the event of damage disputes.

### A few takeaways
{.reflection-heading}

Learn to let go of ideas quickly, regardless of personal attachment. Radiopharmaceuticals matter, but the purpose of EF is to compress the search process. Testing multiple ideas with different people is more effective than prematurely refining a single concept.

Maintain a healthy skepticism when a market gap looks too easy. A clear problem with a straightforward solution and low adoption is rarely an overlooked opportunity. There is usually an underlying structural reason for the lack of adoption, and identifying it before committing is crucial.

Know exactly who your customer is. The existence of a problem does not guarantee that anyone will pay to solve it. A company unable to secure paying customers will not survive long enough to address the core issue.

While starting a company is easier than people generally assume, building a fundamentally sound one is much more difficult. EF makes the distinction between these two realities immediately apparent, which I highly valued.

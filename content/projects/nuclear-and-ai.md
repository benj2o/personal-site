AI is turning electricity into strategy again.

Most conversations about artificial intelligence still orbit models, chips, and talent. That is understandable. They are the glamorous constraints. But if compute demand keeps rising, the less glamorous question becomes impossible to avoid: where does the power come from, and how reliable is it?

This is where nuclear becomes interesting. Not because it is futuristic. Almost the opposite. Nuclear is interesting because it is boring in exactly the way heavy infrastructure needs to be boring: high capacity factor, dense fuel, predictable output, and low operational carbon.

### The Wrong Debate
{.reflection-heading}

The public conversation often treats nuclear as a referendum on optimism. Either it is the clean-energy miracle, or it is a dangerous relic that should have disappeared with the twentieth century.

That framing is not useful. Nuclear is not magic. It is capital-intensive, politically exposed, slow to permit, and unforgiving of institutional weakness. The question is not whether it is emotionally satisfying. The question is whether a serious industrial society can afford to ignore a dense, reliable source of low-carbon electricity while demand is moving upward.

For AI infrastructure, intermittency is not a detail. A training cluster or inference region does not run on vibes. It needs power, cooling, land, interconnection, and regulatory permission. If any of those fail, the model does not care how elegant the architecture is.

### SMRs Are Not a Cheat Code
{.reflection-heading}

Small modular reactors are appealing because they promise repeatability. Build the same unit many times, lower financing risk, standardize construction, and avoid the spectacular cost overruns of bespoke megaprojects.

That story is plausible, but not guaranteed. Modularity only helps after the module exists, supply chains mature, regulators learn the design, and buyers trust delivery. Until then, SMRs are less a product category than a race to make nuclear manufacturable.

I still think the direction matters. Data centers are unusually good potential customers for firm power because they value reliability, can sign long-term contracts, and may be able to colocate near generation. That does not make nuclear easy. It makes the customer problem clearer.

### The AI Angle
{.reflection-heading}

There is a strange irony in building increasingly intelligent systems on increasingly fragile energy assumptions.

If AI becomes economically important, then energy policy becomes AI policy. A country that cannot connect generation, transmission, and datacenters quickly enough will not merely have higher power prices. It will surrender part of the next industrial base.

Europe should be especially honest about this. We cannot simultaneously want technological sovereignty, electrified industry, AI competitiveness, and a power system that treats firm generation as morally embarrassing. Physics does not negotiate with aesthetics.

### My View
{.reflection-heading}

Nuclear deserves less mythology and more operational seriousness.

It will not solve every energy problem, and it will not arrive quickly enough to excuse weak grid planning. But for a world where compute becomes central to defense, medicine, robotics, and scientific discovery, nuclear belongs in the portfolio.

The real test is institutional. Can we permit, finance, build, regulate, and operate complex infrastructure without turning every project into a decade-long proof of national sclerosis?

If not, the bottleneck in AI will not be intelligence. It will be our inability to build the physical world around it.
